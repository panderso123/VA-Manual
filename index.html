How to use this manual

Read Sections 2–4 once. After that, jump straight to whatever project you're working on.

Every project section follows the same shape so it's easy to find your place:

  • What it is — one paragraph so you understand the goal.
  • Your tasks — exactly what you own.
  • Step-by-step — do these in order.
  • Copy-paste prompts — dark boxes. Click Copy, paste into Claude, and replace anything shown in orange brackets with the real detail.
  • Before it goes live — the approval gate. Nothing publishes, sends, or spends money until Peter says yes.
The single most important habit: When in doubt, draft it in Claude, then send it to Peter for a yes/no before it goes public. Drafting is always safe. Publishing and sending are not.

Claude 101 — the basics ↑ top

Claude is an AI assistant. You talk to it like a smart, fast coworker. You'll be on the Max plan, which means generous usage and access to the best models — use it freely.

Getting started in 60 seconds

  1. Go to claude.ai and sign in with the account Peter gives you.
  2. Click New chat.
  3. Type or paste a prompt (an instruction), press Enter.
  4. Read the answer. If it's not quite right, just reply in the same chat and tell it what to change. You do not start over.

Four things that make Claude way more useful

  • Attach files. Click the paperclip / + to upload an image, PDF, spreadsheet, or doc. Claude can read it. (Great for "rewrite this", "pull the key points from this PDF", "describe this product photo".)
  • Keep one chat per task. Everything you say in a chat is "remembered" within that chat. Start a fresh chat when you switch to a different job so context doesn't get mixed up.
  • Use Projects (left sidebar). A Project is a folder of chats that share background info. Peter may set up a Project per business so you don't have to re-explain the brand each time. If so, work inside the right Project.
  • Iterate. The first answer is a draft, not the final. Say "make it shorter," "more casual," "give me 5 options," "now turn it into a caption." This back-and-forth is where the magic is.
Good to knowClaude can be wrong, especially about live facts, prices, dates, links, and anything it can't see. Always sanity-check names, numbers, and URLs before they go out. Treat Claude as a brilliant first-drafter, not a source of truth.

The prompt formula ↑ top

A good prompt has five parts. You don't need all five every time, but the more you include, the better the result. Memorize this order:

PartWhat it meansExample
1. RoleWho Claude should act as"You're a social media manager for a med spa."
2. TaskWhat you want, exactly"Write 5 Instagram captions."
3. ContextBackground, brand, audience"Brand voice is calm and premium. Audience is women 30–55."
4. FormatHow the answer should look"Each caption under 150 characters, with 5 hashtags."
5. ExamplesA sample of what 'good' looks like"Here's a caption we liked: […]"

Here's the formula filled in. This is a real prompt you could use today:

Example — full formulaYou're a social media manager for a premium med spa. Write 5 Instagram captions promoting our [Botox] service. Context: Our brand voice is calm, confident, and premium — never hypey or salesy. Our audience is women aged 30–55 who care about looking natural. We never make medical promises or guarantees. Format: Each caption under 150 characters. End each with a soft call to action and 5 relevant hashtags. Here's a caption style we like: "Subtle. Natural. You, refreshed. Book your consult this week."
If the answer isn't right, just keep talking. Real follow-ups that work: "Make #3 shorter." · "Less salesy." · "Give me 5 more like #2." · "Now write a version for Facebook." You never have to get the prompt perfect on the first try.

10 golden rules of prompting ↑ top

  1. Be specific. "Write a caption" is weak. "Write a 120-character Instagram caption for our lip filler service, calm and premium tone" is strong.
  2. Give the brand voice every time (or work inside the right Project where it's saved). Claude can't read Peter's mind.
  3. Ask for options. "Give me 5 versions" beats one. Pick the best, then refine it.
  4. Show, don't just tell. Paste an example of a post/email you liked and say "match this style."
  5. One task per chat. Don't write captions and plan ads in the same conversation.
  6. Attach the source. Rewriting a doc? Upload it. Describing a product? Upload the photo.
  7. Tell it the format. Bullet list? Table? Under 150 characters? Email with subject line? Say so.
  8. Iterate, don't restart. Refine in the same chat with short follow-ups.
  9. Verify facts. Double-check names, prices, dates, links, medical claims before anything ships.
  10. When unsure, draft & send to Peter. Drafting is free and safe. Publishing/sending needs a green light.

Your daily & weekly routine ↑ top

Daily planHere are my open tasks for today across our projects: [paste your task list] Act as my operations assistant. Group these by project, tell me the order to do them in for the best impact, and flag anything that looks like it needs Peter's approval before I can finish it. Keep it short.

Project: Creative Conquest Top priority ↑ top

A short-form video agency for aesthetic medicine — med spas, dermatology, plastic surgery. Owned by Creative Conquest LLC (a veteran-owned business). Packages: Pilot $1,750/mo · Engine $3,500/mo · Dominance $6,000/mo. This is the highest-profit project, and there's already an operating system built for a VA to run it.

Your two jobs here

Job A — Video production. Move educational/UGC-style videos through the pipeline.
Job B — Outreach support. Help fill the pipeline with prospects and drafted messages.

Job A — The video pipeline (in order)

Each video flows through these stages. Some are software steps Peter will show you; your value is the script, the captions, and quality-checking.

  1. Script — write a 20–40 second educational script about one procedure.
  2. Visual — generated in Higgsfield (Peter's tool).
  3. Voiceover — generated in ElevenLabs from the script.
  4. Lip-sync — done in SyncLabs.
  5. Edit + captions — assembled in CapCut using the saved template.
  6. Post — to the anchor Instagram account, then log the start metrics.

Use Claude for the script — the part that makes or breaks the video:

Video scriptYou're a short-form video scriptwriter for an aesthetic-medicine content agency. Brand voice: disciplined, plain-spoken, educational — no hype, no medical guarantees. Write a 30-second vertical video script that educates viewers about [procedure, e.g. Morpheus8]. Goal: build trust and curiosity so a viewer books a consult. Format: - A scroll-stopping first line (a hook) in the first 2 seconds - 3–4 short spoken lines (conversational, ~55 words total) - A soft call to action at the end - Then list 5 on-screen text overlays and 5 caption hashtags separately Avoid: "guaranteed," "best," before/after promises, or anything a clinic couldn't legally claim.
Compliance — read thisAesthetic/medical content is regulated. Never write guarantees, "cures," specific result promises, or use a real patient's before/after without explicit permission. When unsure, soften the claim and flag it for Peter.

Job B — Outreach support

The agency targets aesthetic-medicine practices with a 7-day free pilot offer, then a 5-touch email sequence. You help by (1) building/cleaning prospect lists and (2) drafting personalized message copy. You draft — Peter sends.

Personalize an outreach email with Claude:

A personal detail to reference: [recent post / press / before-after they shared]. Rules: - Under 90 words - Lead with the personal detail, not us - Offer value, make no hard ask in this first email - 3 subject line options at the top - Plain text, no emojis

Build a clean prospect row from a messy source:

Prospect cleanupHere's raw info I copied about a med spa: [paste anything — website text, listing, etc.] Pull this into a clean table with columns: Practice Name | City | Owner/Contact Name | Hero Procedure | One-line personalization hook | Website | Instagram. If a field is unknown, write "MISSING" so I can find it. Don't guess or invent details.
Where the system lives: Peter's creative-conquest-ops folder has the Client Onboarding Checklist, the Content Production Tracker, the Monthly ROI Report, and the four core SOPs (editing, scheduling, engagement/DMs, prospecting). Ask Peter to walk you through these once — they're built for you to run.

A print-on-demand art brand. Surreal, painterly fine-art cityscapes sold as posters and apparel through Etsy/Printify. The website is already live and 52 products are ready. Brand look: dark gallery aesthetic with gold accents. This is the fastest project for you to own end-to-end — no sales calls, just steady production and listing.

Step 1 — Generate a cityscape

Peter uses Gemini for image generation with a specific brand prompt. Use Claude to perfect the prompt and pick the best output. Here's the brand-correct image prompt — fill in the city and its landmark:

Image prompt (paste into the image tool)A surreal dreamlike oil painting of the [CITY] skyline at night/dusk, featuring [LANDMARK] clearly recognizable. Vivid saturated color, bright and high-contrast, visible impasto brushstrokes, expressive painterly sky, museum-quality fine-art muralist style, semi-realistic with clear detailed forms, balanced natural color (not overly red or orange), no text, no watermark, no logo. Image quality rulesThe landmark must be obviously recognizable — if the city isn't clear, regenerate. Bright, vibrant, high-contrast — if it looks dull or muddy, regenerate. No text, logos, or watermarks in the image. If one appears, regenerate or flag for Peter to crop.

  1. Download the full-size image.
  2. Name it lowercase with underscores: los_angeles.png, denver.png.
  3. Save it to the shared folder Peter gives you. One image per city, portrait orientation.

DESCRIPTION — 2 short paragraphs, premium tone, mentions it's gallery-quality wall art, great as a gift, available as poster and apparel. No fake claims. 3. TAGS — 13 SEO keywords a buyer would search (city name, "skyline wall art", "surreal cityscape", etc.), comma-separated.

Project: The Facebook page & ads ↑ top

The page is new (built recently), so the near-term goal is growth and traffic, not "monetization." A brand-new page can't be monetized yet — that needs scale first (roughly 10,000 followers and an established posting history). Until then, the page's job is to drive people to our links and products.

Your tasks

  • Post consistently (a mix of images, short video, and link posts).
  • Draft boosted-ad copy for Peter to approve and run.
  • Keep everything policy-safe: original art/photos only, no copyrighted music or images.

Why we boost ads (the short version)

A new page reaches almost no one organically. Boosting puts posts in front of people who don't follow us yet — it buys reach and data about what works, cheaply. We boost to grow followers, drive traffic to product links, and learn which posts and products people respond to.

Draft a week of posts

Weekly post batchYou're running the Facebook page for [Sidebush Art — surreal cityscape wall art]. Brand voice: premium, artful, warm. Draft 5 Facebook posts for this week. Mix: 2 showcasing a specific piece ([city names]), 1 behind-the-scenes about the art style, 1 question/engagement post, 1 link post driving to our shop. Format each as: - Post text (2–4 sentences, no more than 1 emoji) - A suggested image idea - A clear call to action Keep it scroll-friendly and never spammy.

Draft copy for a boosted ad

Boost / ad copyWrite 3 versions of a Facebook ad to boost a post for [Sidebush Art]. Goal: [get clicks to our shop / grow page followers]. Audience: [people who love travel, art, and home décor, US, 28–60]. Each version: - A 1-line hook - 2 lines of body - 1 strong call to action - Keep it punchy and mobile-first Then suggest the single best audience-interest targeting for each version.
Money & ad rulesYou may draft ad copy and suggest budgets/targeting, but do not actually launch a paid boost or spend money without Peter's explicit go-ahead each time. Start small (a few dollars/day) only when he approves.
On "monetizing the page": We earn now by driving traffic to products — that doesn't depend on Facebook's monetization program. Facebook's own payout features (in-stream ads, etc.) unlock later once we have the followers and watch-time history. The path there is: post consistently (especially video), boost the best posts to grow followers, stay policy-compliant, and connect a payout method. Don't promise Peter monetization is "switched on" early — it's a milestone we build toward.

Five things to never let Claude talk you into shipping unchecked

  • Government/funding facts (Veterans) without a .gov check.

  • Anything that spends money or hits "publish/send" — that's Peter's call.
